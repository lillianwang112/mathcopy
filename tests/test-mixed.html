1. KaTeX Section KaTeX

In classical mechanics, the Lagrangian $L = T - V$ yields equations of motion via the Euler–Lagrange equation. For a simple pendulum of length $\ell$: $$L = \frac{1}{2}m\ell^2\dot{\theta}^2 - mg\ell(1 - \cos\theta)$$ For small oscillations ($\theta \ll 1$), $\cos\theta \approx 1 - \theta^2/2$, so the period is $T = 2\pi\sqrt{\ell/g}$.

The infinite geometric series: $\sum_{k=0}^{\infty} r^k = \frac{1}{1-r}$ for $|r| < 1$. Setting $r = 1/2$ gives $1 + \frac{1}{2} + \frac{1}{4} + \cdots = 2$.

2. MathJax v3 Section MathJax v3

The wave equation in one spatial dimension: \[\frac{\partial^2 u}{\partial t^2} = c^2 \frac{\partial^2 u}{\partial x^2}\] admits the general d'Alembert solution \(u(x,t) = f(x - ct) + g(x + ct)\), where \(f\) and \(g\) are determined by initial conditions.

The spectral radius of a matrix \(A\) is \(\rho(A) = \max\{|\lambda| : \lambda \text{ is an eigenvalue of } A\}\). By Gelfand's formula: \(\rho(A) = \lim_{n\to\infty} \|A^n\|^{1/n}\).

6. Long Mixed Prose — Select Across Multiple Paragraphs

The central limit theorem (CLT) states that for independent, identically distributed random variables $X_1, X_2, \ldots, X_n$ with mean $\mu$ and variance $\sigma^2$, the normalized sum $$Z_n = \frac{\bar{X}_n - \mu}{\sigma/\sqrt{n}}$$ converges in distribution to the standard normal $\mathcal{N}(0, 1)$ as $n \to \infty$.

This is why many natural phenomena follow bell-curve distributions: any quantity that is the sum of many small, independent contributions will be approximately normally distributed, regardless of the distribution of those contributions — as long as $\sigma^2 < \infty$.

The characteristic function of $X$ is $\varphi_X(t) = \mathbb{E}[e^{itX}]$. For the normal distribution, $\varphi_X(t) = e^{i\mu t - \frac{\sigma^2 t^2}{2}}$. The CLT follows by showing $\varphi_{Z_n}(t) \to e^{-t^2/2}$ pointwise.
